Introduction

Solaris is the guardian of light and energy inside the plant cell. His mission is to absorb sunlight and turn it into glucose, the fuel for life. Without him, the cell would have no energy to grow, heal, or survive.

SOLARIS

Chloroplasts

Chloroplasts are green, energy-making organelles found only in plant and algal cells. They use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to produce glucose, which plants use for energy. They also release oxygen as a byproduct of photosynthesis.



Chloroplasts are oval-shaped and have two membranes. Inside, they contain thylakoids stacked into grana, where chlorophyll captures sunlight. The surrounding fluid, called the stroma, holds enzymes needed for making glucose. They can move within the cell to adjust to light, a process called photorelocation.

Functions in Maintaining Homeostasis
Energy Production: Provide glucose for cell growth, repair, and survival.
Oxygen Release: Produce oxygen, essential for both plants and animals.
Carbon Dioxide Reduction: Help regulate atmospheric gases by absorbing carbon dioxide.
Stress Response: Adjust activity under harsh conditions to protect the cell.



Superpower

Photosynthetic Synthesis — Solaris can capture sunlight and produce unlimited energy in the form of glucose. He also releases oxygen, sustaining life inside and outside the cell.




Weakness

Weaknesses Include:
Lack of Sunlight: Without light, Solaris loses his power and energy production stops.

Environmental Stress: High heat, pollution, or lack of nutrients can weaken him.

Chlorophyll Damage: If his pigments are damaged, he cannot absorb sunlight efficiently.
